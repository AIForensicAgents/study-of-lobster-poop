# study-of-lobster-poop

A concise, professional (and slightly cheeky) repository documenting the observational field study of lobster scat for educational and biological purposes.

---

## Overview

This repository collects field notes, photographs, and interpretive guidance about lobster feces (scat, droppings, feces) as encountered during coastal and subtidal surveys. Its focus is strictly educational and biological: to help naturalists, students, and researchers recognize, record, and responsibly interpret lobster droppings as ecological data points.

What you will find here:
- Field morphology descriptions and quick ID cues  
- Notes on how droppings can reflect diet and habitat use  
- Safety and ethical handling guidance for fieldwork  
- A curated image gallery for training and reference

---

## Why it matters

Understanding scat is a low-cost, low-impact way to gain insight into animal diet, trophic interactions, and habitat use. For lobsters specifically, droppings can:

- Indicate recent meals (e.g., shell fragments, algae, detritus)  
- Offer temporal snapshots of foraging behavior without needing to capture animals  
- Complement gut-content studies and environmental DNA (eDNA) sampling in non-invasive ways

Using scat as data supports conservation-minded research and community science by enabling more observations with less disturbance.

## Morphology notes

Lobster feces vary with species, size, diet, and digestive efficiency. Typical observational notes to record:

- **Form & size:** from elongated strings to short pellets — note dimensions (mm/cm)  
- **Color:** ranging from dark brown/black to greenish or reddish hues depending on recent diet  
- **Texture:** crumbly, fibrous, or compact; presence of mucous coating  
- **Inclusions:** visible shell fragments, setae (hair), algal filaments, undigested prey parts  
- **Odor:** useful descriptively but avoid inhalation — document qualitatively (mild, pungent, marine, etc.)  
- **Context:** location (under ledge, in crevice, on substrate), associated molts, or nearby feeding remains

Record metadata for each sample: date/time, GPS or transect position, tide state, water temperature, and the observer.

## Habitat / Diet clues

Observational clues that help infer habitat use and diet:

- **Shell fragments and chitinous parts:** indicate crustacean predation or scavenging  
- **Algal filaments and plant material:** suggest grazing in algal-dominated habitats  
- **Sediment color/texture within scat:** can reveal benthic foraging in muddy vs. sandy substrates  
- **Frequency and distribution of droppings:** clustering may indicate habitual shelters or feeding stations  
- **Co-occurring biological evidence:** molts, prey carcasses, or foraging scars on bivalves

These are inferential cues—use multiple lines of evidence (direct observation, eDNA, gut analysis when ethical/authorized) before drawing strong conclusions.

## Ethics / Safety

This project prioritizes animal welfare, legal compliance, and researcher safety.

Field ethics
- **Minimize disturbance:** observe from a distance; avoid repeatedly entering or altering shelters.  
- **Permits & regulations:** obtain necessary collection/observation permits from local authorities before formal sampling.  
- **No unnecessary capture:** do not capture or handle lobsters solely for scat collection.

Personal & laboratory safety
- **PPE:** gloves and eye protection when handling samples; wash hands thoroughly after fieldwork.  
- **Biohazard caution:** feces may harbor pathogens—treat samples as potentially biohazardous. Use appropriate containers and disinfect instruments/surfaces.  
- **Disposal:** follow institutional guidelines for biological waste. If in the field, double-bag and carry waste out; do not release concentrated biological material into other habitats.  
- **Transport:** secure samples in sealed, labeled containers; cool as required for downstream analyses.

Respect local communities and property; acknowledge traditional and local ecological knowledge when appropriate.
